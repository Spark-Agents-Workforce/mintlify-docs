**The folklore this lesson kills: "revenue is revenue."**

You pull up a number — this month we did $X — and you reason from it. The problem is that in a subscription business there is no single number called "revenue." There are at least four, they disagree, and the gap between them is where operators quietly lose the plot.

Here's a real, anonymized cautionary tale from the data. **Brand A** — a $50M+ consumables subscription brand — tells its public growth story with one figure. Its actual total-sales ledger, measured straight out of Shopify, runs **roughly 40% higher** than the number in that story. Nobody lied. The two figures simply measure different things — one is a scoped arc, the other is total store sales — and if you'd used them interchangeably in a board meeting you'd have been wrong by tens of percent without touching a spreadsheet.

So before we define a single unit of economics, we install one discipline: **know which ledger you're reading.** Then we build the three numbers that actually decide whether your subscription business is healthy — LTV, payback, and contribution margin per subscriber — on top of that discipline, not underneath it.

## The four ledgers (and why they never match)

When someone says "revenue," ask which of these they mean:

1. **Total store sales.** Every order, subscription or not. This is the biggest number and the one most brands quote. It is *not* your subscription business.
2. **Subscription-only revenue.** The recurring slice. This is the number your investors think they're buying — and it's the one most Shopify data stacks *cannot produce cleanly*. At both case stores the sales ledger is not tag-split: order-count tags exist (you can count subscription orders), but the revenue ledger doesn't separate subscription dollars from one-time dollars. You can say "subscription orders are 41–60% of paid volume"; you cannot yet say "subscription is X% of revenue" without an export nobody has built. Teach the distinction; don't fake the number.
3. **Platform-attributed value.** What Klaviyo or your SMS tool claims it drove. This is directional and it is *not* deduped across channels — the same order can be claimed by email and SMS and paid search at once. **Attribution is not incrementality.** Treat it as a compass, never as the ledger.
4. **The Shopify revenue ledger.** The money that actually settled. This is truth. Everything else is a lens on it.

When your numbers argue, this hierarchy settles it.

## The three units that matter

**Contribution margin per order — the atom.** Import the formula from [the offer math](/growth-program/offer/the-discount-question):

```text
CM/order = (AOV × gross margin %) − fulfillment − payment costs
```

This is the profit a single order actually contributes after the costs that scale with it. The source data is revenue-only — it has no COGS, no fulfillment, no payment costs anywhere. That's not a hole in this lesson, it's the point: no one else's margin can compute yours. You fill this line with your numbers.

**Contribution margin per subscriber — the unit that matters.** Per-order margin is the atom; the subscriber is the molecule. A subscriber isn't one order, it's an *expected stream* of them:

```text
CM/subscriber ≈ CM/order × expected orders per subscriber over the horizon you can defend
```

The horizon is the honest part. Don't model "lifetime" as infinity — model it to a window you can actually observe ([Cohorts](/growth-program/numbers/cohorts) gives you the table that makes this measurable). **The unit is per subscriber, not per order, because every retention decision you make is about the stream, not the transaction.**

**LTV — but the honest version.** Lifetime value is CM per subscriber, carried to your defensible horizon. Note the word *margin*. The most common LTV mistake isn't math, it's ledger: brands compute LTV on *revenue* and call it value. It isn't. Revenue LTV tells you how much a subscriber spends; contribution LTV tells you how much they're worth. Only the second one can be compared to what you paid to acquire them.

**Payback — the clock.** Payback is how long a subscriber takes to earn back their acquisition cost:

```text
Payback (in orders) = CAC ÷ CM/order
```

CAC lives in your ad platforms; our source data has zero spend/CAC/ROAS, so payback is a framework you fill, not a number we can quote. An experience range, marked as opinion not data: a consumables subscription that pays back inside the first two-to-three orders is comfortable; one that needs five-plus is living on borrowed cohorts.

## The proof — what the units look like in the wild

The data can't give you margins, but it can show you the *shape* of subscriber value, which is why these numbers are worth chasing.

- **AOV clusters tightly.** Both case stores sit in a **$92–115 monthly AOV band** — remarkably close for two different categories. Roughly-$100 orders are the raw material both LTV and payback are built from. (Consistent across months; monthly aggregate, not customer-level.)
- **A subscriber stream dwarfs a single order.** At **Brand B** — a $35M+ snacks brand with a paid annual membership — members were worth **~$875 in lifetime revenue across ~6 orders each** — and even members who eventually *cancelled* still averaged **~$690 lifetime**. Member order value ran **~40–49% above the untagged baseline**. (Hedges, inline and non-negotiable: this is a *self-selected* cohort measured against an aggregate untagged remainder, not a matched non-member control; it's lifetime-to-date, not a closed lifetime; one measured case, directional, not incrementality.)
- **The trap, made concrete.** Notice that even the store's own best figure — ~$875 — is a *revenue* number. With no cost inputs it cannot become contribution LTV. The brand with the richest subscriber data in our register still can't hand you a true LTV. That's not a data failure to hide; it's the exact lesson: **the units are only as honest as the ledger under them.**

One measured case is not a law. What these rows license is a single claim: a retained subscriber is worth a large multiple of a single order — which is why the whole rest of this program exists.

## The artifact — the unit-economics one-pager

Fill this in for your own store. It is the sheet you bring to every other numbers lesson.

```text The one-pager
WHICH LEDGER (check one per figure you cite)
  [ ] Total store sales   [ ] Subscription-only   [ ] Attributed   [ ] Shopify ledger

INPUTS
  AOV ................................ $______
  Gross margin ...................... ______%
  Fulfillment + payment / order .... $______
  Expected orders / subscriber ..... ______ (to a horizon you can defend)
  CAC (blended, from your ad platforms) $______

THE THREE UNITS
  CM/order        = (AOV × GM%) − fulfillment − payment   = $______
  CM/subscriber   = CM/order × expected orders            = $______
  LTV (contribution, not revenue)                          = $______
  Payback (orders) = CAC ÷ CM/order                        = ______ orders

THE ONE HONESTY CHECK
  Is my LTV built on margin or revenue? __________
  Is my "revenue" total-store, subscription-only, or attributed? __________
```
