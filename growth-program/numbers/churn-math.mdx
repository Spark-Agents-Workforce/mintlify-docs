**The folklore this lesson kills: "our churn is 5%."**

One number, stated with confidence, and it tells you almost nothing you can act on. Churn is not one number — it's at least four, stacked into a single figure that averages away exactly the information you'd use to fix it. A blended churn rate is a thermometer that reads the whole house at once: it can tell you something's warm, never which room is on fire.

This lesson splits the blend into its parts, because **each part routes to a different fix.** Get the split and you know what to do Monday. Keep the blend and you'll spend a quarter treating a billing problem like a loyalty problem.

## Split one: logo churn vs revenue churn

**Logo churn** counts *subscribers* lost — heads out the door, as a percentage of the base.

**Revenue churn** counts *recurring revenue* lost — dollars out the door, as a percentage of MRR.

They diverge the moment your subscribers aren't identical, which is always. (The definitions and the divergence logic here are framework, not a measured row.) Lose your smallest subscribers and logo churn runs *higher* than revenue churn — you're shedding heads but keeping dollars. Lose your biggest and revenue churn runs *higher* than logo churn — a few cancellations, disproportionate damage. Watching only logo churn, you can congratulate yourself on a stable base while your best accounts quietly walk.

There's a third figure worth naming: **net revenue churn**, which subtracts expansion (upgrades, add-ons, quantity increases) from the revenue you lost. When expansion outruns losses, net revenue churn goes *negative* — the holy grail: the surviving base grows even with no new customers. You can't chase what you don't measure separately.

## Split two: voluntary vs involuntary

This is the split most brands never make, and it's the one that pays the fastest.

**Voluntary churn:** the subscriber *chose* to leave. Cancelled, didn't reactivate. This is a value or experience problem — the product, the price, the cadence, the onboarding.

**Involuntary churn:** the subscriber *didn't choose* to leave. Their card expired, the payment failed, the retry logic gave up. They may not even know they're gone. This is a billing and operations problem — dunning sequences, card-account updater, smart retry timing.

Two diseases, two treatments. And here's the number that makes the split urgent: involuntary churn is commonly a large minority of gross churn, often on the order of 20–40% — marked as operating experience, not a figure from our data. If a third of your churn is failed payments, the highest-ROI retention work in your building isn't a win-back email, it's a dunning flow. You will never see that from a blended rate.

(Neither case store's data separates voluntary from involuntary churn — there is no cancel-reason export and no dunning/failed-payment export in the evidence. We can teach the split and why it matters; we cannot quote a real involuntary share from the data. We won't invent one.)

## Why the blended number lies — with real event volumes

The data lets us watch the raw churn machinery at one store, and it makes the case perfectly.

At **Brand A** (the \$50M+ consumables brand), a single half-year window logged roughly **37,000 cancellation events** against about **1,200 reactivations** and **700 resumes.** Read as one blended number, that looks like a five-alarm fire — churn swamping saves by more than 30-to-1.

Now read it un-blended, and the story inverts:

- **Net creation stays positive every month.** Monthly subscription *creates* consistently exceed *cancels* — the base grows on net despite enormous gross churn. Churn here is a volume to dampen, not an existential threat. (Hedge: these are lifecycle *event counts*, not distinct-subscriber churn; creates and cancels aren't deduped to customers; there's no tenure data.)
- **The save motion is real and accelerating.** The save/resume ratio climbed from **~1.3% to ~5.7%** across roughly nine months — a small number quintupling. (Hedge: event volume only — the data shows *that* saves happen and are growing, never *which* offers or UX drove them; earlier near-zero months are an instrumentation limit, not a true absence.)
- **And even this can't tell you the two things that matter most.** Was that 37,000 mostly voluntary or mostly failed payments? Were the lost subscribers whales or minnows? The blended event count answers neither.

## The artifact — the churn decomposition worksheet

Run your last full month through this. The point is to never write a single churn number again without its four parts beside it.

```text The worksheet
PERIOD: __________

LOGO CHURN
  Subscribers at start ............ ______
  Cancels (chose to leave) ........ ______   → VOLUNTARY
  Failed-payment losses ........... ______   → INVOLUNTARY
  Logo churn %  = (cancels + failed) ÷ start = ______%
    of which voluntary: ____%   involuntary: ____%

REVENUE CHURN
  MRR at start .................... $______
  MRR lost to churn .............. $______
  Expansion MRR (upgrades/add-ons) $______
  Gross revenue churn % ........... ______%
  NET revenue churn % (after expansion) ..... ______%   (negative is the goal)

THE ROUTING (what each row sends you to)
  Voluntary high  → value/experience (onboarding, offer, saves)
  Involuntary high → billing ops (dunning, card updater, retry timing)
  Revenue >> logo  → you're losing your biggest — segment and defend the top
```

<InOpenSubs>Involuntary churn gets its own line because failed payments are handled by smart retries and dunning built into the platform — so the number you read is real churn, not billing you could have recovered with a retry.</InOpenSubs>
