**The folklore this kills: "more campaigns = more revenue."**

Here is the reflex almost every subscription brand has when revenue needs to move: send another campaign. Another Tuesday blast, another sale, another "we miss you." It feels like work, so it feels like progress. The measured case in this module says the reflex is wrong — not a little wrong, structurally wrong. At the anonymized supplements brand we studied, broadcast campaign value stayed essentially flat year over year (roughly \$28M to roughly \$29M in attributed value) *while recipients grew by around 70%*. Revenue per recipient fell from about \$0.66 to about \$0.40. They sent to far more people and got the same money. Meanwhile the automated flows — the messages triggered by what a subscriber *did* — grew about two-thirds, from roughly \$4.6M to roughly \$7.6M in attributed value over the same year. (Platform attribution, not incrementality; summed recipients are not unique people.)

That is the whole thesis of this module in one contrast:

More campaigns is not the lever. The lever is having the *right message waiting at every moment a subscriber can be in* — so the message fires itself, forever, without you touching the calendar.

This anchor lesson gives you that map. Every message a subscriber should ever get, from the moment they hand over an email to the moment they lapse and you try to win them back — on one page. It is deliberately a **swipe-file index**, not copy. We do not have the brand's actual headlines or body text — the evidence contains triggers, message counts, and performance, never the words. What we *can* give you, grounded in real flow shapes, is far more durable than headlines anyway: for each moment, the **job** that message does, the **shape** the evidence shows works, and **what good looks like** as a number you can compare your own program against.

## The decision: build the moments, not the calendar

Run this on your own store. Print the map below and mark, in a third column, which moments you currently have a message waiting in. Most operators discover they have three or four of the ten well-built and the rest either missing or stuffed into a single generic "post-purchase" afterthought. The gap analysis *is* the roadmap — you build the empty rows in order of the revenue-per-recipient they command, not in the order they're easiest.

Two structural rules the evidence teaches before the table:

1. **Efficiency lives at high-intent moments; scale lives at broad ones.** Rev-per-recipient peaks on tight triggers (abandoned checkout at ~\$6.15/recipient) and craters on broad ones (broadcast at ~\$0.40). Do not judge a delivery flow by the same yardstick as a promo blast — they have different jobs.
2. **The map is only as complete as your tags.** In the measured store, one flow worth roughly \$1.3M attributed had *no trigger label at all*. A "complete" lifecycle map is complete up to what your platform actually tagged — audit for orphan flows.

## The artifact: the lifecycle map

Attribution throughout is platform-attributed, not incrementality-tested. Shapes are inferred from trigger + message-count, not creative. "What good looks like" is one store's real number — a reference point, not a target.

| Moment / trigger | The job | Shape the evidence shows | What good looks like (one measured case) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **List signup** (added to list) | Convert a new lead into a first-time buyer | ~6 messages, **front-loaded** — message 1 carries the clearest conversion path, then educate and de-risk | ~\$1.65/recipient; a single front message can carry **~70% of the flow's value**; ~0.50% unsub |
| **Browse abandonment** (viewed, no cart) | Re-surface interest; educate, don't discount | ~7 messages, soft, category/education-led | ~\$1.17/recipient — lowest intent; leading with a discount here wastes margin |
| **Abandoned cart** (added, no checkout) | Reduce uncertainty; guide back to checkout | ~22 messages, broader, lower-urgency | ~\$4.86/recipient; top message ~\$9.36/recipient |
| **Abandoned checkout** (started, no purchase) | Highest-pressure recovery — they were *this* close | ~13 messages, **tight**: a hero early, then warm, lighter follow-ups | **~\$6.15/recipient — the highest of any flow**; hero message ~\$9.45/recipient |
| **Post-purchase** (order placed) | Educate, reinforce usage, open the 2nd-purchase path | ~20 messages, **sustained** — wins on breadth, not one hero email | ~\$0.87/recipient across huge volume (~\$2.2M total); the largest single engine by *scale* |
| **Post-delivery** (order delivered) | "Product in hand": usage, replenishment, review, cross-sell | **Only ~2 messages** — the tightest high-value flow | ~\$0.88/recipient at **0.26% unsub — the lowest friction of any flow** |
| **Pre-charge / upcoming order** (renewal approaching) | Prevent churn: transparency before the card is hit | 1 reminder (SMS shines here — see [SMS](/growth-program/messaging/sms)) | ~\$5.85 rev/msg at **0.09% unsub**, ~350x dashboard ROI |
| **Churn-risk** (behavioral risk signals) | Many targeted saves at many risk moments | **~57 messages** spread across branches — *not* one save-blast | ~\$1.2M spread thin; **no single dominant message** — model as branches |
| **Post-cancel** (subscription cancelled) | A direct reactivation path | ~4 messages | ~\$0.75/recipient, small (~\$32k) — keep it, but it can't do the churn flow's job |
| **Winback / lapsed** (dormant, broadcast) | Reactivate the long-cold | Campaign, not flow | ~\$0.50/recipient; **no holdout — directional only** |

## The proof, read honestly

The shape of that table is the argument. Notice the two flows with the cleanest efficiency — abandoned checkout (~\$6.15/recipient) and post-delivery (2 messages, 0.26% unsub) — are *small* in message count. Notice the two biggest engines — post-purchase (~20 messages) and churn (~57 messages) — win by *breadth*, spreading value across many messages with no hero. That is the tell that these are systems, not blasts. A single generic "post-purchase email" cannot do the work of a 20-message education engine plus a 2-message delivery flow plus a 57-branch churn system, no matter how good the copy.

And the hedges are the curriculum here, not fine print. Every dollar is platform-attributed and may overlap other channels; the shapes are inferred from structure, not read from the copy; the day-offsets inside each flow are yours to set, not ours to prescribe. An operator who can hold "this is attributed, not incremental" in their head while still acting on it is a better operator than one who either prints the number naked or freezes because it isn't a randomized trial.
