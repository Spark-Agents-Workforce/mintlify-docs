**The folklore this kills: "SMS is email but shorter."**

If SMS were just short email, you'd write your emails, trim them, and send. Brands that do this burn their list — because SMS is a fundamentally different contract. Email is a channel a subscriber lets pile up; SMS is a tap on the shoulder in their pocket. That intimacy is exactly why SMS *out-earns* email per message on the right jobs, and exactly why it *destroys* your list on the wrong ones. This lesson is about which messages earn the tap on the shoulder.

The headline from the measured case: over a 60-day window (a snapshot, not a trend), SMS attributed roughly \$2.6M at **43.9x dashboard ROI**, 0.50% unsub. Real money. But the *average* ROI hides the whole lesson, because SMS performance is almost entirely a function of *who you send to* and *why*.

## The audience-quality ROI ladder

Rank the same channel by audience and the spread is enormous (all dashboard attribution — not incrementality, not deduped; one store, 60-day window):

| SMS audience | Dashboard ROI | What it is |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Prospecting / broad sends | ~**8x** | Sending to people who barely know you. Real but the floor. |
| Active-subscriber sends | ~**118x** | Sending to people already paying you. ~15x better than prospecting on the same channel. |
| Behavioral automations (checkout/cart/browse) | ~**338x** | Firing on what someone just did. Returning-customer checkout recovery hit up to ~\$19.49 per message. |

That ladder is the whole strategy. **SMS beats email precisely where intent and relationship are highest** — behavioral triggers and active subscribers — and loses its shirt as you push it toward cold prospecting. The single strongest lifecycle row of all was the **pre-charge reminder: ~\$5.85 per message, 0.09% unsub, ~350x ROI.** The near-zero unsub is the tell — the right SMS at the right moment doesn't feel like marketing at all.

**The attribution hedge, kept inline because it changes how you read the ladder:** these are *dashboard* multiples. They almost certainly overlap email and other channels — a subscriber may get the reminder by SMS *and* email and convert once, credited twice. So do not read "338x" as "\$338 of new money per dollar." Read the *ladder* — the *relative* ordering of prospecting vs subscriber vs behavioral is the durable, decision-grade signal; the absolute multiples are inflated by non-dedupe. (Separately, popup-capture SMS shows huge attributed revenue per message, but it's *capture* attribution, not directly comparable to normal sends — so it's deliberately kept off this ladder.)

## Timing, MMS, and the honest caveats

**Timing.** The case shows midweek and evening/early-morning sends over-indexing on revenue per message — Wednesday and ~6PM lead. Treat this as **observational, not causal**: the tables have no embedded date range and simply sum volume by slot; evening slots also carry higher unsub pressure. Use it as a hypothesis to test on your own list, not a rule to obey.

**MMS.** In the evidence, every observed MMS part sat inside *automation flows* (a welcome series was the MMS-heaviest), with none in the broadcast-campaign detail. The read: **MMS is a lifecycle enrichment tool** — rich creative for a welcome or delivery moment — not a broadcast toy. Image-heavy blasts are where SMS costs spike without the intimacy premium.

## The artifact: the SMS decision tree — which messages earn SMS

Walk every candidate message through this. If it doesn't clear the gates, it stays on email.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Is it time-critical or behavior-triggered?">
    (Pre-charge reminder, shipping, checkout recovery, failed payment.) **Yes: strong SMS candidate** — these are where SMS's per-message ROI concentrates. No: next gate.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Is the recipient an active subscriber or high-intent — not a cold prospect?">
    No, cold/broad: **keep it on email.** (~8x floor vs ~118x for subscribers — SMS's premium evaporates on cold audiences.) Yes: next gate.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Can it be said in ~1–2 short lines with one clear action?">
    No (needs images/long copy): if it's a *welcome or delivery* moment, consider **MMS inside a flow**; otherwise email. Yes: next gate.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Does it respect consent and cadence?">
    (Consented opt-in, quiet hours, not stacking on top of three other texts this week.) No: **fix consent/cadence first** — see [Deliverability & cadence](/growth-program/messaging/deliverability-and-cadence). Yes: **send SMS.**
  </Step>
</Steps>

The decision that clears all four every time is the **pre-charge reminder** — behavioral, subscriber, one line, low-frequency. That is why it's the strongest SMS row in the data. The decision that fails gate 2 every time is the promotional blast to your whole list — which is exactly the send most brands reach for first.

**One measured case, one channel window.** This is a single store's 60-day SMS snapshot on a dashboard. The *shape* of the ladder is the teaching; your list's consent quality sets where the threshold actually falls.
