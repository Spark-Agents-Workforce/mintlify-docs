**The folklore this kills: "the calendar is the strategy."**

Ask most brands to see their marketing strategy and they'll show you a calendar — a grid of the sends going out this month. That is a *broadcast* strategy, and the measured case shows exactly what a broadcast strategy does at scale: it plateaus. Recall [the anchor's](/growth-program/messaging/lifecycle-map) headline contrast — broadcast value flat year over year while recipients grew ~70% (rev/recipient \$0.66 → \$0.40), against flows growing about two-thirds (\$4.6M → \$7.6M attributed). The calendar is not the strategy. **The flow architecture is the strategy;** the calendar is just what you layer on top of it for the promo windows.

This lesson is the architecture: how to build flows that compound, why they beat broadcasts on a per-message basis, and how to segment a *subscription* list so the right subscriber gets the right trigger.

## Flows vs campaigns: what "compounds" actually means

A campaign is a one-time send to a list. Its value is realized once and then it's over; to get more value you send again, to more people, and — as the case shows — you hit diminishing returns. A flow is a message attached to a *behavior*. You build it once and it fires every time any subscriber does the triggering thing, forever. Its value *accrues*. That is the entire difference and it is the entire reason the flow line on the chart bends up while the campaign line goes flat.

The per-message evidence makes it concrete. On SMS in the measured store, **automation flows earned ~\$3.10 per message (117x dashboard ROI)** against **broadcast campaign sends at ~\$0.46 per message (23x)** — the same channel, a ~6.7x efficiency gap, purely from *triggered* vs *blasted*. (Dashboard attribution, one store; bodies and holdouts absent.) A flow reaches a subscriber at the moment the message is relevant to them; a broadcast reaches everyone at the moment convenient to you. Relevance is the whole premium.

This does **not** mean stop sending campaigns. It means campaigns are the *amplifier layer* for real promo windows, not the engine. And even inside campaigns, the case kills a second piece of folklore: **editorial out-earned sale.** Educational/editorial campaigns attributed roughly \$6.8M against roughly \$4.7M for major-sale campaigns — about 1.5x more, from *fewer* sends (35 vs 87). (Selection-biased toward top-attributed rows — directional, not a full taxonomy.) The calendar mind assumes "campaign = sale." The data says your best-performing broadcasts may be the ones that teach, not the ones that discount.

## The metric-triggered flow set

Build these in this order — they map directly onto [the lifecycle map](/growth-program/messaging/lifecycle-map), now expressed as the *triggers* your ESP listens for:

- **Added-to-list → welcome** (front-loaded; ~\$1.65/recipient in the case).
- **Checkout started, no order → abandoned checkout** (the highest-RPR recovery, ~\$6.15/recipient — build this before abandoned cart).
- **Added to cart, no checkout → abandoned cart** (~\$4.86/recipient).
- **Viewed product, no cart → browse abandonment** (education-led, ~\$1.17/recipient).
- **Order placed → post-purchase** (the ~20-message education engine; the scale play).
- **Order fulfilled/delivered → post-delivery** (2 messages, lowest unsub — the highest-efficiency retention moment).
- **Upcoming charge → pre-charge reminder** (the churn-prevention workhorse from [Transactional & reminder](/growth-program/messaging/transactional-and-reminder)).
- **Risk metric crosses threshold → churn flow** (branch it — the case spread ~\$1.2M across ~57 messages, not one).
- **Subscription cancelled → reactivation** (small but keep it).

One honest caveat before you trust any flow taxonomy: in the measured store a flow worth ~\$1.3M attributed carried *no trigger label*. Audit for orphan flows — the "complete" set is only as complete as your tags.

## Segmentation for subscriptions

Consumer-brand segmentation folklore is demographic — age, gender, geography. For a subscription brand that is almost noise. **The segments that move retention are behavioral and lifecycle:** tenure (first-order vs month-3 vs month-12), product (what they're subscribed to, which sets replenishment timing), and status (active vs paused vs cancelled vs at-risk). The one place the evidence speaks *directly* to segmentation value is SMS audience quality: **active-subscriber sends returned ~118x dashboard ROI against ~7.6x for broad prospect sends** — the same channel, ~15x apart, purely on who was targeted. (We lack email segment-level performance, so this is the cleanest measured segmentation signal we have; dashboard attribution.)

Segment on what predicts the next order — tenure, product cycle, status — and let demographics go.

## The artifact: the flow-set blueprint

A one-page build order. For each flow: the trigger, the job, and the "don't" that kills it.

| Build order | Trigger | Job | The "don't" |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Added to list | First purchase | Don't stop at a signup confirmation — front-load the conversion path |
| 2 | Checkout started, no order | Recover highest intent | Don't bury the hero message late — it fires first |
| 3 | Order placed | Educate + 2nd purchase | Don't cram it into one email — it's a sustained engine |
| 4 | Delivered | Product-in-hand retention | Don't treat it as a transactional afterthought |
| 5 | Upcoming charge | Prevent churn | Don't make it a pitch — transparency + skip/swap |
| 6 | Cart / browse | Softer recovery | Don't lead with discount on browse (lowest intent) |
| 7 | Risk metric | Branched saves | Don't send one generic save-blast |
| 8 | Cancelled | Reactivation | Don't expect it to do the churn flow's job |

**Smart-sending caution:** ESP "smart sending" and quiet-hours windows can silently suppress a time-critical reminder. Exempt genuinely time-sensitive flows (pre-charge, failed payment, delivery) from smart-send throttles.
