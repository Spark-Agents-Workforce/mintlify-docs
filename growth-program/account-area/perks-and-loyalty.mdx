**The anti-lesson this kills: "more perks = more loyalty."**

Here is the folklore, and it is expensive. *"Loyalty is a stack. Add points, add a birthday gift, add free shipping, add a members' discount, add a surprise-and-delight box — every perk you bolt on makes them a little less likely to leave."*

It feels right. It is mostly wrong, and it fails in a specific, quiet way:

  <p className="m-0" style={{ fontWeight: 900, fontSize: "20px", lineHeight: 1.3, textTransform: "uppercase", color: "#000" }}>An unmeasurable perk is a margin leak wearing a loyalty costume.</p>
</div>

It shows up on the P&L every month as given-up margin, and it shows up in retention as… nothing you can point to. You *assume* it's working because churn didn't spike. You can't prove it, so you never cut it, so it bleeds forever.

The job of this lesson is not to talk you out of perks. It's to make you *audit* them — to separate the two or three perks that actually move renewal from the pile that just costs you money and wears the loyalty word as cover.

> This is the running-perks lesson. Whether to launch a paid membership *at all* is a different decision — that's [Memberships & access passes](/growth-program/offer/memberships). Here we assume you already have subscribers and a perk stack, and we're going to grade it.

## The decision: the cost-per-retained-month test

Every perk is a trade. You give up something — margin, cash, ops effort — to buy something — a retained member-month. So grade each perk on two axes:

1. **What does it cost you, per member, per month?** Cash-back likely to be redeemed, discount margin conceded, shipping subsidy, the amortized cost of a signup or referral credit, the ops load of a surprise box. Put a monthly number on it.
2. **What is the retention *mechanism*, and can you measure it firing?** Not "it makes them happy" — *what specific behavior does this perk cause that keeps them subscribed?* And can you see that behavior in your data?

That second axis is the whole lesson. **A perk you can't measure is a perk you can't defend.** If you can name the mechanism *and* watch it happen, the perk earns its cost. If the mechanism is vague and invisible, you are paying for a feeling — and "more perks = more loyalty" is how that feeling justifies itself right up until you tally the annual bleed.

## The proof: one perk that measures itself — the credit float

Here's a measured case where the mechanism is not only nameable, it's *countable*. **Brand B** — the eight-figure snacks brand — runs a paid membership (~\$60/year) whose main perk is **store credit** — a few percent cash-back on member orders, plus signup and referral credits. Read the hedges; they're the curriculum.

Watch what store credit does that a plain discount can't:

- **It builds a return-trip float.** At the snapshot, the program had issued roughly **\$925K in credit and about two-thirds of it — ~\$600K — was still outstanding** (lifetime-to-date balance; no per-credit redemption timing exists, so this is a standing balance, not a redemption curve). Outstanding credit is two things at once: a liability on your books, and a standing, *named* reason for each member to come back and spend. It's a leash you can see.
- **It touches an enormous slice of orders.** Credit-tagged orders across the window touched roughly **\$14.5M over ~96.5K orders** (the credit order-tag is broader than the membership's own credit ledger and isn't reconciled to it — so read it as "credit is pervasive in how these people buy," not as a clean membership number).
- **The mechanism is visible.** You can watch outstanding balance rise as credit is earned and fall as members return to redeem. That is a retention mechanism you can *measure firing* — the exact opposite of "we think the birthday gift helps."

Now the discipline. This is **one measured case**, and two hedges travel with it everywhere. First, members spent about **+48% more per order** than the untagged remainder — a real signal, but there's no holdout: members self-select, so credit didn't manufacture a big spender out of a tire-kicker; it gave your already-deep buyers a reason to route spending back to you. Second, we have no COGS/margin data — we can show credit's *return-loop behavior*, but you must supply the margin cost to complete the test.

And the case tells you *when* perks matter most. Joins were easy — steady at roughly a thousand-plus a month — but **renewals (rebills) lagged badly**, growing several-fold year-over-year off a small base as the first annual cohorts came due (timing, not a churn rate). Read that plainly: **getting the join is not the hard part; earning year two is.** The entire job of your perk stack is to make the *second* year worth paying for. Even members who eventually cancelled had averaged roughly **\$690 in lifetime revenue** — the floor is high, which is exactly why leaking margin on unmeasurable perks is such a waste: these are valuable people, so spend the perk budget on the two or three levers you can prove.

## The artifact: the perk audit worksheet

List every perk you offer. Fill a row for each. The thresholds are a starting line calibrated to one case, not a law.

| Perk | Cost / member / month | Retention mechanism (name the behavior) | Measurable? | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| *(e.g.) Store credit / cash-back* | *conceded margin on redeemed credit* | *outstanding balance = named reason to return; you watch it redeem* | **Yes — balance + redemption visible** | **KEEP** |
| *(e.g.) Members' standing % off* | *margin on every member order, forever* | *"they feel like they're saving" — no distinct behavior* | **No** | **CUT / redesign** |
| *(e.g.) Free shipping for members* | *shipping subsidy per order* | *only real if it beats a threshold most orders already clear* | **Test it** | **AUDIT** |
| *(e.g.) Early access to drops* | *~zero hard cost; ops + scarcity* | *pulls members to buy in a window; visible as a signup/order spike* | **Yes — window spike** | **KEEP** |
| *(e.g.) Surprise-and-delight box* | *COGS + fulfillment per box* | *goodwill — no isolable retained-month* | **No** | **CUT / instrument first** |

**How to read your filled sheet:**

- **Measurable? = No** → you cannot defend the spend. Either instrument the mechanism so you *can* measure it, or cut it. Every month you keep it, you're paying for a feeling.
- **Cost well under the retained value, mechanism visible → KEEP.** These are your load-bearing perks. Fund them harder.
- **Cost roughly equals or exceeds value, or mechanism vague → CUT or redesign.** Prefer perks that create a *return loop* (credit that must be spent with you) over perks that are pure giveaway (a standing discount is a slow, permanent margin bleed that re-creates the discount-subscription trap).

Run it before your next cycle. Most operators find one perk they can prove, and two or three they've been quietly funding on faith.
