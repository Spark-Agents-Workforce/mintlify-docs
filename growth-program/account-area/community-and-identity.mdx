**The anti-lesson this kills: "community is a Discord."**

The folklore here is a purchase order. *"We need community. Spin up a Discord. Add a members' forum. Start a Facebook group. Community: acquired."*

That is community as *theater* — a room you built and hope people move into. Sometimes they do. Usually you get a ghost server, three superfans, and a moderator you're now paying.

Only *then* does a gathering place have anything to gather around. The tell is simple: real membership identity changes how people behave with your *whole brand*, not just inside the walled garden. If your "community" only exists in the Discord and vanishes at the storefront, you built a room, not an identity.

## The decision: the identity ladder

Membership identity is a climb, not a switch. Four rungs, and you have to *earn* each before the next is real. (The ladder and its tests are a framework, not a measured finding.)

1. **Transactional** — they buy the product. That's it. No relationship beyond the receipt.
2. **Perks** — they get member pricing, credit, points. A *reason* to prefer you, still fundamentally a discount relationship (see [Perks & loyalty](/growth-program/account-area/perks-and-loyalty) — and note perks alone don't create identity, they create preference).
3. **Access** — they get things non-members *can't* get: gated products, early windows, member-only drops. Now there's a line between in and out, and being "in" means something.
4. **Identity** — being a member is part of *how they see themselves*. They buy across your whole catalog, they recruit friends, they'd wear the shirt. The membership is a badge, not a coupon.

**The earn-it test at each rung — ask before you claim to be there:**

- **To claim Perks:** can a member name what they get, and does it change their preference? If they can't name it, you're still Transactional with extra costs.
- **To claim Access:** is there something real behind the wall that people specifically *want* — and can't get elsewhere on your site? If the "access" is a 5% code everyone eventually finds, there's no wall.
- **To claim Identity:** do members behave like members *across your whole catalog*, not just at the gate? Would they recruit a friend unprompted? If the only evidence of "community" is a server you pay to moderate, you're performing identity, not earning it.

You climb by making the *product experience* worth belonging to. The Discord, if it ever makes sense, is a rung-4 amenity — never the ladder itself.

## The proof: identity drives full-catalog behavior

Here's the measured signal that identity is real and not theater — from **Brand B**'s membership (the eight-figure snacks brand). The mechanism color is qualitative, so read it as one case's texture, not a law.

The brand gates a small slice of desirable, member-only products behind the membership. You'd expect, if the membership were "just the gated stuff," that members mostly buy the gated SKUs. **The opposite is true.** Gated/member-only products were only about **27% of member spend** — the other **~73% was the regular catalog every customer can see** (product-type is a proxy; member orders include ordinary purchases). But flip it: those gated products were about **84% bought by members.**

That inversion is the whole lesson. **The gated cuts are the *hook*, not the *volume*.** A small, scarce, members-only slice creates the line between in and out — and then membership changes how those people shop your *entire store*, not just the gated shelf. That's identity: it drives full-catalog behavior. One anchor gated product alone was roughly half of gated sales, doing exactly the job an identity hook is supposed to do — giving "being a member" a concrete, desirable meaning.

Two more textures point the same way. Membership signups **peaked around 4.5% of orders during limited product-drop / access windows** — noticeably above the steady ~3% baseline, and *not* during blanket discount events (coincidence, not incrementality — no holdout exists). **Scarcity and access recruited members better than markdowns did.** And member-only early-access sends earned a premium response at near-zero unsubscribe pressure — the audience *wants* the insider signal. People don't opt harder into a discount; they opt harder into belonging.

Note what this is *not*: there's no Discord in this data, no forum, no measured "community" surface at all. The identity shows up as **behavior** — full-catalog spend, drop-window pull, early-access appetite — earned in the product experience. That's the point. You don't buy this with a server; you earn it with something behind the wall worth being inside for.

## The artifact: the identity ladder + earn-it test

Place your subscription honestly, then decide what to build to climb.

| Rung | You're here if… | The earn-it test to climb to it | What it costs to fake (the theater trap) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1 · Transactional** | relationship ends at the receipt | — | — |
| **2 · Perks** | members get pricing/credit/points | can a member *name* the perk and does it shift preference? | perks nobody remembers = margin leaks |
| **3 · Access** | members get things others can't | is there something *wanted* behind the wall, unavailable elsewhere? | a "gate" everyone gets past = no wall |
| **4 · Identity** | membership is part of who they are | do they buy full-catalog + recruit unprompted? would they wear the shirt? | a paid-for Discord standing in for earned belonging |

**How to use it:** find your true rung (be strict — most brands claiming rung 4 are at rung 2 with a logo). Then climb *one* rung by earning its test, not by buying its amenity. If you're at Perks, the move isn't a community server — it's putting something real behind an access wall (rung 3). If you're at Access and want Identity, the move is making membership *mean* something across the whole experience, so the behavior spreads past the gate.

> Where this touches [Memberships](/growth-program/offer/memberships): that lesson decides *whether* to sell access behind a fee. This lesson is about the *identity* that makes the sold access sticky — and about not mistaking a chat room for the thing.
