**The anti-lesson this kills: "subscriptions sell themselves from the PDP."**

If [the widget](/growth-program/store/pdp) is perfect, the temptation is to stop there — the PDP will do the work. It won't, because a huge share of your buyers never take the path you imagine. They don't land on a hero PDP, read the widget, and decide. They enter on the home page or a collection, browse, and buy — and if the subscription path isn't merchandised into *that* journey, they meet the choice for the first time cold, deep in a product page, with no framing. Subscriptions don't sell themselves from the PDP. They sell from a *path* you build across the store, and the collection and nav layer is where that path is drawn.

## The decision

Merchandising the subscription path is three connected calls. (The doctrine, the collection-vs-badge ruling, and the nav-weight calibration are frameworks from building these stores; the data has no merchandising A/B to rank them.)

**Call 1 — findability.** Every subscribable product should signal that it's subscribable *before* the click, and there should be at least one obvious route to "the subscription version of this store." The failure mode is a store where subscription is invisible until you're already on a PDP — which means everyone who buys from a collection grid never knew the option existed until the last second.

**Call 2 — dedicated collection vs badges vs both.** The honest ruling depends on how much of your catalog subscribes:

- **Badges** (a small "Subscribe & save" flag on collection tiles) when *most* products are subscribable — a dedicated collection would just re-list the whole catalog and teach nothing.
- **A dedicated collection** ("Shop subscriptions" / "Build your routine") when subscription is a *subset* or a distinct experience — it gives the path a front door, a landing surface you can link from the nav, the announcement bar, and the why-subscribe page.
- **Both** for most stores at scale: badges to signal on the grid, a dedicated collection to give the path a home. The two do different jobs — one *labels*, one *routes*.

**Call 3 — navigation weight.** How prominent the subscription path sits in the nav should match your attach *goal*, not your enthusiasm. A brand whose whole model is subscription earns a top-level nav slot; a brand testing subscription on a few SKUs earns a badge and a collection link, not a takeover. Over-weighting the nav on a low-attach catalog just clutters the path to the products people actually came for.

## The proof (and its honest edge)

The data cannot tell you that a dedicated collection lifts attach — no such row exists. What it proves is *where entry revenue concentrates*, which is the whole reason this layer matters.

At Brand A, GA4 shows the **home page and the catalog collection page carrying the bulk of converting sessions** — home ~6.0% session CVR on the largest session volume in the store, and the products/collections page ~6.8% CVR, the single highest-converting major surface *(GA4 directional traffic, not ad economics)*. Hero PDPs convert well too (4.6–6.4%), but home and collections are where the *volume* of the buying journey lives.

Fixing the widget alone leaves the highest-traffic surfaces silent on subscription.

Second proof, again from concentration: Brand A's demand resolves to a **few hero packages** across every window. That tells you what the dedicated collection should lead with — not an exhaustive A-Z of every subscribable SKU, but the hero routine, merchandised first. A subscription collection that opens with your three best bundles converts the browser; one that opens with 90 tiles makes them leave.

Honest edge: this is entry-surface evidence, not path-attach evidence. The claim it licenses is "merchandise subscription where the traffic is," not "a dedicated collection lifts attach by X" — that X does not exist in the data.

## The artifact — the findability audit

Walk your own store as a first-time visitor and answer each. Any "no" is a hole in the path.

```text The audit
ENTRY SURFACES (where the traffic actually lands)
  [ ] Home page states subscription exists (a section, a hero, or a nav item)
  [ ] Main collection/shop page signals subscribable products (badges on tiles)
  [ ] The three hero products are reachable from home in one click

THE SUBSCRIPTION FRONT DOOR
  [ ] There is ONE obvious route to "shop subscriptions" (nav item or hero link)
  [ ] The dedicated collection (if used) leads with hero bundles, not the full catalog
  [ ] Its landing copy answers "why subscribe" in the first screen

SIGNAL ON THE GRID
  [ ] Collection tiles for subscribable items carry a consistent "Subscribe & save" badge
  [ ] The badge states the benefit, not just the word ("Save 15%" / "Never run out")

NAV WEIGHT (match to your attach goal, not your hope)
  [ ] Nav prominence matches how much of the catalog subscribes
  [ ] The path isn't so heavy it buries the products people came to browse

THE COLD-VISITOR TEST
  [ ] A stranger who lands on home and never opens a PDP still learns subscription exists
  [ ] A stranger who lands on a collection can start a subscription without hunting
```
