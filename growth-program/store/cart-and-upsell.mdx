**The anti-lesson this kills: "upsells are free money."**

The pitch every upsell app sells you is that upsells are pure upside — bolt one on, revenue goes up, no cost. That is the most expensive belief in this module, because every upsell you add to the cart is a decision you force on a buyer who had *already decided to buy*. Get it right and the cart is a second, softer chance to attach the one-time buyer who slipped past the PDP. Get it wrong and you've inserted friction at the exact moment of highest purchase intent — and friction there doesn't cost you the upsell, it costs you the *order*. Upsells are not free money. They are a trade, and you have to know what you're trading.

## The decision

Three cart moves, each with a friction budget. (The in-cart upgrade design, the threshold sizing, and the bump-vs-friction rule are frameworks from building these carts; the data has no in-cart attach row. The lifecycle rows below illustrate the *cost of friction*, not the *lift of a bump*.)

**Move 1 — the in-cart subscription upgrade, done right.** This is the highest-value cart move: a one-time buyer sits in the cart, and you offer "Make it a subscription and save 15%" as a single toggle that upgrades the line item in place. Done right it is *one tap, no re-add, no navigation* — the item they already chose, upgraded, with the saving shown inline. Done wrong it's a pop-up modal that interrupts the checkout march, or a swap that empties and rebuilds the cart.

**Move 2 — threshold offers.** "Add $X for free shipping" / "Spend $Y, get a gift." These nudge AOV *up* — the opposite of what a discount does to the same order (see [Free gifting](/growth-program/offer/free-gifting)) — and they're most powerful when the threshold is set just above your current AOV, close enough to feel reachable. The failure is a threshold set so high it reads as a tax, or a threshold that distracts from the subscription upgrade you actually want. One offer at a time in the cart; two competing offers cancel each other.

**Move 3 — order bumps vs friction.** An order bump is a pre-checkout add-on ("add the travel size?"). A bump is worth it *only* when it's genuinely relevant to what's in the cart and adds zero steps. Every irrelevant bump, every extra tap, every modal is a withdrawal from a friction budget you cannot see until conversion drops. The discipline: **one relevant bump maximum, and only if it clears the friction budget.** When in doubt, cut it — the order you protect is worth more than the bump you might win.

## The proof (and its honest edge)

The data has no in-cart upgrade attach numbers. What it has is a loud, measured lesson about how much the cart-to-checkout moment is worth — which is exactly why you protect it.

At Brand A, the **abandoned-checkout recovery flow earns the highest revenue-per-recipient of any lifecycle flow in the store — ~$6.15 per recipient, with its hero message at ~$9.45** *(platform-attributed, not incrementality-tested)*. Read that backwards: the reason abandoned-checkout recovery is the single most valuable-per-person flow is that the people who reach the cart and *don't* finish are enormously valuable and enormously close. Anything you add to the cart that increases abandonment is destroying the most valuable recovery pool you have. Friction in the cart isn't a lost upsell; it's a lost near-buyer.

Second proof, on the flip side — cart-stage interventions *do* work when they're behavior-triggered and relevant: Brand A's **behavioral cart/checkout SMS automations returned ~337.9x ROI, with returning-customer checkout recovery as high as ~$19.49 per message** — but at a **higher unsubscribe rate** than any other flow *(attribution only; higher unsub pressure noted in-source)*. That is the whole lesson of this lesson in one row: interventions at the cart moment are high-value *and* high-cost. They work, and they carry friction. You run them anyway — carefully, one at a time, watching the unsub and the abandonment as the price of admission.

Honest edge: these are recovery-flow and SMS rows, not in-cart-bump rows. They prove the cart moment is precious and that friction there is expensive. They do not put a number on any bump's lift — that number doesn't exist.

## The artifact — the cart decision tree

Run every proposed cart element through this before it ships.

```text The decision tree
START: A one-time item is in the cart. What do you want most?
  → Almost always: ATTACH IT (upgrade to subscription). Everything else is secondary.

THE IN-CART UPGRADE (do this first)
  Is the "make it a subscription" upgrade ONE tap, in place, no re-add?
    NO  → fix that before adding anything else. This is the highest-value move.
    YES → is the saving shown inline, in the customer's terms?
            NO → add it.  YES → ship it. Move on.

ADDING A THRESHOLD OFFER?
  Is there already an active offer competing for attention in the cart?
    YES → stop. One offer at a time. Kill the weaker one.
    NO  → is the threshold set just above current AOV (reachable, not a tax)?
            NO → resize it.  YES → ship it.

ADDING AN ORDER BUMP?
  Is it genuinely relevant to what's in THIS cart?
    NO  → cut it. Irrelevant bumps spend the friction budget for nothing.
    YES → does it add any steps, modals, or taps to reach checkout?
            YES → cut it or make it zero-step.
            NO  → one bump maximum. Ship it.

THE FRICTION CHECK (run after any change)
  Did cart→checkout conversion or abandonment move the wrong way?
    YES → the last thing you added is the suspect. Remove it. The order > the upsell.
```
