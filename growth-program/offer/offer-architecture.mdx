**The folklore this lesson kills: "the offer is the discount."**

Ask an operator "what's your offer?" and nine times out of ten you'll get a percentage back. Ten percent off. Twenty. First month free. The offer, in most people's heads, *is* the discount — a single number you turn up when growth is slow and down when margin gets tight.

That's the number. The number is a decision you make **later**, inside a model you should have chosen **first**. This lesson is about the model. Architecture is the shape of the deal — subscribe-and-save, paid membership, prepaid or commitment, bundle, build-a-box, trial-to-sub — and the shape decides which direction your economics move before you ever pick a percentage. Choose the wrong architecture and no discount number rescues it. Choose the right one and the number becomes a tuning problem, which is a good problem to have.

Here's the whole point in one contrast, drawn from two real stores in our data.

## Two spines, opposite economics

**Spine one — the discount subscription.** **Brand A** (the same store you'll meet again in [The discount question](/growth-program/offer/the-discount-question)) runs a subscribe-and-save architecture: a first-order discount to start, a modest standing rate to stay. Recurring orders make up roughly **41–60% of its paid orders** month to month — the recurring base *is* the business. And the fastest lever it has on acquisition mix is offer depth: when it moved the first-order subscription offer to 25%, daily subscription starts rose **~53%** and subscription first-orders went from ~14% to ~20% of all paid orders within days *(acquisition-window signal; lift in starts, not proven downstream quality)*. Notice what a discount subscription does to AOV: it pushes it **down**. That's the instrument working as designed — you concede margin on every order to buy frequency and commitment.

**Spine two — the paid membership.** **Brand B** — the eight-figure snacks brand behind [Memberships](/growth-program/offer/memberships) — runs a paid-membership architecture: a **~$60/year fee** that unlocks access, credits, and gated products. No standing percentage off. Member-tagged orders run roughly **+48% higher AOV** than the untagged remainder *(members self-select; the baseline is an aggregate remainder, not a matched non-member cohort, and there's no holdout — attribution, not proven lift)*. Members are worth about **$875 each** across ~6 orders, and the membership's share of a *growing* store climbed from **~13.5% to ~26%** over two years. The tell: even **cancelled** members averaged roughly **$690 in lifetime revenue** — about **7×** a single baseline order (~$96) *(self-selected, lifetime-to-date)*. A membership pushes AOV **up** — you charge for belonging and monetize access.

One lowers price to buy frequency; the other charges for access and sells more at full price. Same DTC scale, mirror-image economics. Your architecture decides which way your AOV moves — and you decide that *before* you pick a number.

## The fit logic

Architecture isn't taste; it's a match between the deal's shape and the product's natural rhythm. (The routing below is a framework from operating experience, not a measured row — run it on your own product, not ours.)

The three inputs that decide it: **product type × AOV × consumption cadence.**

| If your product is… | …the architecture that fits |
| --- | --- |
| Consumable, predictable refill cadence, mid AOV | **Subscribe-and-save** (discount subscription) → [The discount question](/growth-program/offer/the-discount-question) for the number |
| High repeat frequency + a gate-able desirable slice + a deep catalog behind it | **Paid membership** → [Memberships](/growth-program/offer/memberships) for the viability test |
| Consumable but cash-flow-sensitive, gift-heavy, or strongly seasonal | **Prepaid / commitment** → [Prepaid & commitment](/growth-program/offer/prepaid-and-commitment) |
| Variety-seeking, low loyalty to any single SKU | **Build-a-box / bundle** |
| High-consideration, real trial friction | **Trial-to-sub** |

The anti-pattern is any model that fights the product's rhythm: a membership on a brand nobody buys from twice a year, a discount subscription on a product people genuinely want to *vary*, a prepaid plan on an impulse SKU. When the architecture fights the rhythm, no number fixes it — you just discount your way to a churn problem.

## One layer down: codes do jobs, too

Even inside a single architecture, the offer isn't one thing. In the discount-subscription store, the discount **codes** split cleanly by job when you classify them by their first-time-buyer share: acquisition codes ran **~87% first-time** buyers, while recurring and group codes ran **76–86% recurring** (one group code was ~100% subscription) *(same-order tags are proxies; they don't prove future cohort retention)*. The lesson that travels: **classify a code by its first-time% before you judge it.** Comparing an acquisition code to a retention code on one KPI is how good offers get killed for the wrong reason.

## The artifact — the architecture-fit worksheet

```text The worksheet
STEP 1  DESCRIBE THE PRODUCT
  Product type ................ consumable / variety / considered
  AOV ........................ $______
  Natural repeat cadence ..... every ____ weeks (or "irregular")
  Repeat occasions / year .... ______

STEP 2  READ THE RHYTHM → CANDIDATE ARCHITECTURE
  Predictable refill, mid AOV .............. → subscribe-and-save
  Buys often + gate-able slice + deep catalog → membership
  Cash-flow / gift / seasonal .............. → prepaid/commitment
  Variety-seeking .......................... → build-a-box / bundle
  Trial friction ........................... → trial-to-sub

STEP 3  CHECK THE DIRECTION OF AOV
  Discount subscription → AOV goes DOWN (buying frequency). Fund it?
  Membership → AOV goes UP (selling access). Frequency high enough?

STEP 4  ONLY NOW PICK A NUMBER
  → route to the model's lesson. The number lives THERE, not here.
```
