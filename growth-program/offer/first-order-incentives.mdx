**The folklore this lesson kills: "we're 20% off."**

Most brands run one number. That 20% acquires the customer *and* it's what they pay on every renewal forever — one dial, set once. It feels clean. It's the most expensive mistake in subscription offer design.

The acquisition offer and the ongoing rate are **two separate decisions**, and the gap between them — the **cliff** — is a design choice you're making whether you know it or not. Set a deep first-order offer over a modest standing rate and you've built a cliff: order one is cheap, order two costs meaningfully more. That price jump lands at exactly the moment of highest churn risk in the whole subscription lifecycle — [the second order](/growth-program/first-60-days/second-order-cliff). Manufacture a steep enough cliff and you've *built* your own second-order churn, then blamed the customer for "just wanting the deal."

This lesson is about designing the two dials, and the descent between them, on purpose.

## The two-dial timeline (measured)

The discount-subscription store in our data — **Brand A** — runs the two dials openly, and the platform timeline lets us watch a first-order move in near real time. (Figures are acquisition-window signals; lift in starts, not proof of downstream quality.)

- **The move:** it took the first-order subscription offer to **25%** over a standing ongoing rate of **~10%** — a 15-point cliff.
- **The acquisition result:** daily subscription starts rose **~53%**, and subscription first-orders climbed from **~13.7% to ~20.1%** of all paid orders within days. First-order depth is the fastest mix lever in the entire dataset.
- **The holiday variation:** during a subsequent 10-day holiday window, the store **reverted to 10% initial + 10% recurring** (no cliff) with a separate promo code stacked on top — a completely different offer structure that has to be analyzed on its own terms, not lumped in with the 25% test.
- **What held through the switches:** the **recurring share of subscription orders stayed roughly 74–80%** across the changes *(derived: in the holiday window ~26,240 sub orders carried ~6,854 first-orders, so ~74% were recurring; monthly order-tag slices, not a cohort curve)*. The existing base kept re-ordering while the acquisition dial moved — which is the point: **moving the first-order dial doesn't touch the people already past it.**

And the verdict everyone actually wants — did the 25% cliff acquire customers who *stayed*? The 25% cohort's 180-day retention matures in late 2026. Until it lands, the +53% start lift is a hypothesis about volume, not a verdict on quality. (This is the same open cohort flagged in [The discount question](/growth-program/offer/the-discount-question) — the two lessons share it.)

## How steep is too steep

The cliff's cost isn't the first-order margin — that's a bounded, one-time cost you already decided to pay. The cliff's cost is the **churn it induces at the price jump.** A subscriber who signed up at 25% off and now faces the full ongoing rate on order two experiences a price *increase* precisely when they're deciding whether this subscription is worth keeping.

There's no single safe cliff height in the data — you have to read it off your own second-order retention. (The descent designs below are frameworks; the data proves the two-dial *structure* and the acquisition lift, not a specific optimal cliff.) Three ways to manage the descent:

1. **The honest single cliff.** Deep first order, then straight to the standing rate. Simplest; works when your product's value is obvious enough by order two that the price jump feels fair. Watch second-order retention like a hawk.
2. **The stepped descent.** 25% → 15% → 10% across the first three orders. Softens the jump so no single renewal is a shock. Costs more margin over orders two and three; buys second-order survival. Use when your single-cliff second-order retention is bleeding.
3. **The gift-cushioned cliff.** Keep the price cliff but land a [milestone gift](/growth-program/offer/free-gifting) on order two or three. The customer's *perceived* value stays high across the jump while your price anchor holds — a gift costs COGS, not a permanent margin point.

Design the descent so the customer reaches the ongoing rate before they'd have churned anyway.

## The artifact — the two-dial descent blueprint

```text The blueprint
STEP 1  SET THE TWO NUMBERS
  First-order offer ...... ______%   (bounded, one-time; buys starts)
  Ongoing rate ........... ______%   (compounds forever; the LTV number)
  Cliff = first-order − ongoing = ______ points

STEP 2  MEASURE YOUR SECOND-ORDER RETENTION TODAY
  % of first-order subscribers who take order two = ______%
  (This is your cliff's report card. Low here = cliff too steep OR value too slow.)

STEP 3  CHOOSE THE DESCENT
  Second-order retention healthy .......... → honest single cliff
  Second-order retention bleeding ......... → stepped descent (25→15→10)
  Want to hold price anchor + lift value .. → gift-cushioned cliff

STEP 4  WATCH THE RIGHT NUMBER
  Not starts (those already rose). Watch ORDER-TWO retention by cohort,
  60-day on-pace read → 180-day verdict. Move ONE dial, then wait.
```
